Several industries now run reinforcement learning in production, not just in simulation. I focus on systems where RL operates continuously in the field, affecting millions of real decisions, such as ride-hailing dispatch at DiDi, data center cooling at Google, hotel revenue management, and financial order execution. The chapter concludes with a simulation study on the bus engine replacement problem, demonstrating that RL matches and surpasses dynamic programming on a classical benchmark.

\subsection{Ride-Hailing Dispatch}
\label{sec:ridehailing}

Each driver-passenger assignment changes the spatial distribution of available drivers, making ride-hailing dispatch a sequential optimization problem at a scale (tens of millions of daily rides at DiDi) where exact dynamic programming is intractable.

\citet{Qin2021dispatch} formalized DiDi's order dispatching as a semi-Markov decision process\footnote{A semi-Markov decision process generalizes the standard MDP by allowing variable time between decisions. The discount factor $\gamma^{\tau_k}$ depends on the actual time elapsed $\tau_k$ rather than applying a fixed per-step discount.} where each driver is an independent agent. The state of a driver consists of location (discretized into hexagonal zones) and time (bucketed into intervals). The action is the order assigned to the driver, with the option to remain idle. The reward is the trip fare. State transitions are determined by trip destinations, as completing an order transports the driver from origin to destination, changing their spatial state. The stochasticity arises from future demand, which determines the available actions at each state. The per-driver value function $V^{\pi}(s)$ represents the expected cumulative fare a driver can earn from state $s$ under dispatching policy $\pi$:
\begin{equation}
V^{\pi}(s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^{\tau_k} r_k \mid s_0 = s, \pi \right],
\end{equation}
where $\tau_k$ is the time to complete the $k$-th trip and $r_k$ is the corresponding fare. The platform's objective is to maximize total driver income across the fleet, which decomposes into the sum of individual driver value functions.

The deployed system uses generalized policy iteration within a combinatorial optimization framework. Each dispatching window (a few seconds), the platform collects open orders and available drivers, constructs a bipartite graph, and solves a linear assignment problem. The edge weights are computed as the advantage of each driver-order match relative to the driver's current state value:
\begin{equation}
w_{ij} = \hat{r}_i + \gamma^{\hat{\tau}_i} V(s_j') - V(s_j),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{r}_i$ is the predicted fare for order $i$, $\hat{\tau}_i$ is the estimated trip duration, and $s_j'$ is the destination state. The value function is learned via temporal-difference methods from historical trip data. DiDi's Cerebellar Value Network \citep[CVNet;][]{Tang2019cvnet} uses hierarchical coarse-coding\footnote{Coarse-coding represents a continuous state using overlapping receptive fields at multiple resolutions. At coarser levels, each tile covers a large region, capturing broad spatial patterns; finer levels capture local detail. The value function is the sum of features activated across all resolution levels.} with a multi-resolution hexagonal grid, enabling transfer learning across cities and robustness to data sparsity in low-traffic zones.

Production deployment across more than 20 Chinese cities demonstrated consistent improvements. Table~\ref{tab:didi_results} summarizes the reported gains from A/B tests using time-slice rotation, where algorithms alternate control of the platform in 3-hour blocks to avoid interference effects.

\citet{Li2019ridesharing} addressed the coordination challenge using mean-field multi-agent RL. With thousands of drivers making simultaneous decisions, the full multi-agent state space is intractable. The mean-field approximation replaces individual driver states with an aggregate distribution, allowing each driver to condition on the density of nearby drivers rather than their exact locations. Experiments on DiDi data showed improved fleet utilization compared to single-agent baselines.

\citet{Han2022lyft} reported complementary results from Lyft, where the dispatching system optimizes driver assignment and repositioning jointly. Their value decomposition architecture\footnote{Value decomposition decomposes the platform's global matching objective into individual driver value functions, each estimable via temporal-difference learning. The global optimum is recovered by optimizing the sum of individual values.} assigns credit to individual driver decisions within the global objective. The production system demonstrated improvements in rider wait times and driver utilization, confirming that the RL dispatch paradigm generalizes across platforms and regulatory environments.

The percentage improvements appear modest, but at the scale of ride-hailing operations they translate to substantial aggregate effects. A 1\% improvement in fulfillment rate at DiDi's volume represents hundreds of thousands of additional completed rides per day. The central lesson from these deployments is that dispatching is not just a matching problem but a fleet positioning problem. Today's assignments determine tomorrow's driver distribution.


\subsection{Data Center Cooling}
\label{sec:datacenter}

Data centers consume enormous amounts of energy, with cooling systems accounting for 30--40\% of total power usage. Google applied machine learning to autonomous HVAC control, achieving substantial efficiency gains while maintaining temperature safety constraints. The problem is a continuous control task with high-dimensional state and action spaces, strict safety requirements, and non-stationary dynamics.

\citet{Gao2014} reported initial results from deploying neural network predictions to guide data center operations. The system used over 100 sensor readings, including temperatures at multiple locations, coolant flow rates, power draw, and external weather conditions, to predict Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE).\footnote{PUE is the ratio of total facility energy to IT equipment energy. A PUE of 1.0 means all energy goes to computing; the industry average is approximately 1.6.} Operators used these predictions to adjust equipment setpoints. This assisted approach achieved a 40\% reduction in cooling energy and a 15\% improvement in overall PUE.

\citet{Lazic2018} subsequently deployed a fully autonomous system using model-predictive control with learned dynamics. The state space consists of temperatures, flow rates, and equipment states across multiple zones. Actions are setpoints for chillers, fans, and pumps. The system learns a neural network dynamics model $\hat{f}$ from operational data:
\begin{equation}
s_{t+1} = \hat{f}(s_t, a_t) + \epsilon_t.
\end{equation}
At each control interval (5 minutes), the system optimizes over a horizon of future states using the learned model, subject to temperature constraints:
\begin{equation}
\min_{a_0, \ldots, a_H} \sum_{h=0}^{H} c_{\text{energy}}(a_h) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad T_{\min} \leq T_{\text{zone},i}(\hat{s}_h) \leq T_{\max} \; \forall i, h.
\end{equation}

The MPC approach provides interpretability and safety guarantees that pure model-free RL cannot. Actions are projected onto the feasible set defined by temperature constraints, with uncertainty estimates from the learned model used to maintain conservative margins. Human operators can override at any time. Table~\ref{tab:datacenter_results} summarizes the reported performance.

\subsection{Hotel Revenue Management}
\label{sec:hotel}

Hotels face a dynamic pricing problem with perishable inventory, since rooms unsold by check-in yield zero revenue. Classical revenue management uses booking limit controls derived from dynamic programming under parametric demand assumptions. RL offers an alternative that learns pricing policies from data without specifying demand functional forms.

The problem is a finite-horizon MDP. The state $(t, I, d)$ consists of days until check-in, remaining rooms, and a demand intensity indicator. Actions are discrete price levels. Booking arrivals follow a Poisson process with intensity depending on demand conditions; each arrival books with probability decreasing in price. The objective is to maximize total revenue:
\begin{equation}
V_t(I, d) = \max_{p \in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{E}\left[ p \cdot B + V_{t-1}(I - B, d') \mid I, d, p \right],
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the number of bookings at price $p$ and $d'$ is the next-period demand state.

\citet{Gallego1994pricing} established the analytical benchmark for dynamic pricing with Poisson demand. Under their intensity control framework, the optimal price trajectory can be characterized when demand follows specific functional forms. The Gallego-van Ryzin solution provides a theoretical upper bound, and any practical pricing system should approach this performance in settings where the model assumptions hold.

\citet{Chen2023hotelrl} applied deep Q-learning to hotel revenue management, training on simulated booking data under parameterized demand models. The DQN agent uses state features (normalized time, normalized inventory, demand indicators) and outputs Q-values for each price level. Table~\ref{tab:hotel_results} compares performance against baselines on held-out test periods.

It computes booking limits by comparing the expected marginal revenue of reserving a unit for a higher fare class against selling it now.} Training time is nearly constant in hotel capacity, whereas DP computation grows linearly. The learned policies exhibit interpretable patterns, with prices increasing as inventory depletes and as check-in approaches, with larger adjustments during high-demand periods.


\subsection{Financial Order Execution}
\label{sec:execution}

Optimal execution of large equity orders requires balancing execution risk (adverse price movements while holding inventory) against market impact (price deterioration from trading aggressively). The theoretical benchmark is the Almgren-Chriss framework \citep{Almgren2001execution}, which derives optimal deterministic schedules under linear impact assumptions. Real markets exhibit state-dependent liquidity that these assumptions miss.

\citet{Nevmyvaka2006execution} applied tabular Q-learning to execution on real limit order book data from NASDAQ stocks (AMZN, QCOM, NVDA). The state consists of time remaining, inventory remaining, bid-ask spread, and signed volume imbalance.\footnote{The limit order book records all outstanding buy and sell orders at each price level. Signed volume imbalance is the difference between buy and sell order volume near the best prices, normalized by total volume; positive imbalance typically predicts short-term price increases.} Actions are trade sizes or the decision to wait. The reward is the negative implementation shortfall,\footnote{Implementation shortfall measures the total cost of executing a trade relative to a benchmark price, typically the mid-quote at order arrival. It captures market impact, timing cost, and opportunity cost of unexecuted shares.} the difference between execution price and a benchmark. The agent learns to condition on market microstructure signals, trading aggressively when spreads are narrow, wait when order flow predicts favorable price movement, accelerate near the deadline.

\subsection{Supply Chain Inventory Management}
\label{sec:inventory}

Multi-echelon inventory systems coordinate ordering decisions across supply chain stages, where each stage's order becomes the next stage's incoming shipment. A retailer orders from a warehouse, which orders from a distribution center, which orders from a factory. The sequential nature creates complex dependencies, since an order placed upstream today affects downstream availability many periods later. The state space grows exponentially in the number of echelons, with $K$ stages each having $M$ possible inventory levels yielding $M^K$ states. Classical inventory theory provides elegant solutions for special cases, most notably the echelon base-stock policy of \citet{ClarkScarf1960inventory}.

The MDP formulation for a serial $K$-echelon system is as follows. The state $s = (I_1, \ldots, I_K)$ records on-hand inventory at each stage, where stage 1 faces customer demand and stage $K$ is the most upstream. The action $q \in \{0, 1, \ldots, Q_{\max}\}$ is the order quantity placed at stage $K$. Demand $D_t \sim F_D$ arrives at stage 1 each period; unfilled demand is backordered. Shipments flow downstream, with stage $k$ receiving what stage $k+1$ shipped in the previous period. The per-period cost combines holding, backorder, and ordering components:
\begin{equation}
c_t = h \sum_{k=1}^{K} I_k^+ + b \cdot (D_t - I_1)^+ + c_o \cdot q_t,
\end{equation}
where $I_k^+ = \max(0, I_k)$ is on-hand inventory, $(x)^+ = \max(0, x)$, $h$ is holding cost per unit, $b$ is backorder cost per unit, and $c_o$ is ordering cost. The objective is to minimize expected discounted total cost.

\citet{Gijsbrechts2022inventory} conducted a systematic evaluation of deep RL against classical base-stock policies across lost sales, dual sourcing, and multi-echelon configurations. The classical benchmark is the echelon base-stock policy, in which each stage $k$ maintains an echelon inventory position and orders to bring this position to a target level $S_k$.\footnote{Formally, the echelon inventory position at stage $k$ equals on-hand inventory at $k$ plus all inventory at downstream stages $1, \ldots, k-1$ plus in-transit inventory, minus backorders. The echelon base-stock policy of \citet{ClarkScarf1960inventory} is optimal for serial systems with linear costs and backorders.} For single-echelon systems with backorders, the optimal base-stock level is given by the newsvendor critical fractile $S^* = F_D^{-1}(b/(b+h))$. The results are sobering for RL proponents. In settings where classical solutions exist and can be computed, RL adds little value and often underperforms. The base-stock policy, derived analytically for idealized settings, remains highly competitive when properly calibrated. RL struggles particularly with multi-echelon coupling, where orders at upstream stages affect costs at downstream stages many periods later, creating difficult credit assignment problems.\footnote{Credit assignment refers to the difficulty of determining which past actions caused a delayed reward or cost. In multi-echelon systems, an upstream ordering decision today may not affect customer-facing costs for many periods, making it difficult for RL to learn the causal connection.} The authors report that DRL required millions of training transitions to approach performance levels that base-stock policies achieve through closed-form calculation. The value proposition for RL in inventory management lies in problems where analytical solutions are unavailable, such as non-stationary demand, complex operational constraints, or settings where the cost structure does not admit tractable decomposition.


\subsection{Real-Time Bidding}
\label{sec:rtb}

Real-time bidding for display advertising presents a budget pacing problem in which an advertiser must allocate a fixed budget across a sequence of auctions to maximize conversions. \citet{Wu2018rtb} formalized this as an MDP where the state includes remaining budget, time elapsed, and recent win rates, with actions being bid multipliers. The RL agent learns to adapt bidding intensity to market conditions, increasing bids when competition is low and conserving budget when prices spike. Simulation experiments calibrated to real RTB data showed improvements over rule-based pacing algorithms.


\subsection{Simulation Study: Bus Engine Replacement}
\label{sec:bus_engine}

I conclude with a simulation demonstrating that RL matches dynamic programming on a classical economics benchmark. The bus engine replacement problem, introduced by \citet{Rust1987}, models a fleet manager's monthly decision whether to replace engines based on accumulated mileage. Replacement incurs a fixed cost but resets mileage to zero; continued operation incurs maintenance costs increasing in mileage.

I extend the single-engine problem to a fleet of $N$ engines with a capacity constraint limiting replacements per period. The state $s = (m_1, \ldots, m_N)$ records discretized mileage for each engine. Actions are subsets of engines to replace, subject to the capacity constraint. The per-period cost is $c(s, a) = \alpha \sum_{i} m_i + \beta |a|$, where $\alpha$ is the operating cost per unit mileage and $\beta$ is the replacement cost.\footnote{The mileage-dependent operating cost $\alpha \sum_i m_i$ follows Rust's original specification $c(x, \theta_1) = \theta_{11} x$, creating a non-trivial threshold replacement policy. The fleet extension uses deterministic mileage increments to isolate the combinatorial scaling challenge that arises from the joint state of multiple engines.} Mileage evolves deterministically; replaced engines reset to $m = 0$; others increment by one bin. With $M = 6$ mileage bins, the state space is $6^N$, yielding 1,296 states at $N = 4$, 7,776 at $N = 5$, 46,656 at $N = 6$. Value iteration is feasible for $N \leq 5$.
