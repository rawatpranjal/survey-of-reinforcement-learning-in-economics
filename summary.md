# Summary: Overcoming the Curse of Dimensionality in Reinforcement Learning and Dynamic Programming

The "curse of dimensionality" presents a fundamental impediment to the scalability and tractability of Reinforcement Learning (RL) and Dynamic Programming (DP) problems. This phenomenon manifests as an exponential increase in computational and sample complexity with the growth of state-action space dimensions. The surveyed literature explores various theoretical and algorithmic approaches to address this challenge, encompassing structural assumptions, function approximation techniques, and sophisticated sampling strategies.

## Key Contributions from the Literature:

### Du et al. (2021) - "Bilinear Classes: A Structural Framework for Provable Generalization in RL"
This work introduces the **Bilinear Class** as a unifying structural framework for achieving provable generalization in RL, particularly when tackling large state-action spaces via function approximation. It subsumes and extends existing models, such as Bellman Rank and Bellman Complete frameworks, incorporating new settings like Linear Q*/V* and RKHS versions of linear MDPs. The Bilinear Class framework posits that the Bellman error can be bounded by a bilinear form, allowing for efficient data reuse across hypotheses—a parallel to uniform convergence in supervised learning. The proposed BiLin-UCB algorithm achieves polynomial sample complexity, notably reducing it to the generalization error of an underlying supervised learning problem. This framework is characterized by its non-parametric rates, expressed through critical information gain, demonstrating applicability in infinite-dimensional RKHS settings where explicit feature dimensions are not required.

### Liu et al. (2022) - "Understanding Deep Neural Function Approximation in Reinforcement Learning via $\epsilon$-Greedy Exploration"
This paper investigates the theoretical underpinnings of deep neural function approximation within RL, specifically focusing on $\epsilon$-greedy exploration in an online setting, motivated by the success of Deep Q-Networks (DQN). The authors analyze value iteration algorithms with deep (and two-layer) neural networks, considering target Q-functions residing in **Besov and Barron function spaces**. They demonstrate that sublinear regret can be achieved with finite network width and depth. For deep neural networks in Besov spaces, a width scaling of $\tilde{O}(T^{d/(2\alpha+d)})$ and depth $O(\log T)$ are shown to be sufficient. For two-layer networks in Barron spaces, a width of $\Omega(\sqrt{T})$ suffices, crucially avoiding the "curse of dimensionality" with respect to feature dimension $d$. A key technical contribution is the reformulation of temporal difference (TD) error estimation in an $L^2(d\bar{\mu})$-integrable space, transforming it into a generalization problem under non-i.i.d. data settings, often mitigated by experience replay.

### Lu et al. (2025) - "Overcoming the Curse of Dimensionality in Reinforcement Learning Through Approximate Factorization"
This research proposes **approximate factorization** of MDPs into smaller, independently evolving components as a provable strategy against the curse of dimensionality. Unlike traditional factored MDPs, this scheme accommodates imperfectly factorized models, offering greater flexibility. A **multi-component factorized synchronous sampling** approach is introduced, framed as a cost-optimal graph coloring problem, enabling simultaneous updates for multiple components from a single sample. This leads to model-based and model-free (Variance-Reduced Q-Learning with Approximate Factorization, VRQL-AF) algorithms that achieve improved problem-dependent sample complexities. Notably, the sample complexity scales with the maximum component size rather than the sum, demonstrating an exponential reduction in dependency on state-action space size when the MDP is factorizable. The framework also identifies a trade-off between sample complexity and model misspecification bias, influenced by the level of factorization.

### Nguyen-Tang et al. (2021) - "On Sample Complexity of Offline Reinforcement Learning with Deep ReLU Networks in Besov Spaces"
This paper addresses the statistical theory of **offline RL** with deep ReLU network function approximation in Besov spaces. It establishes a sample complexity of $n = \tilde{O}(H^{4+4d/\alpha} \kappa_{\mu}^{1+d/\alpha} \epsilon^{-2-2d/\alpha})$, considering novel concepts like **Besov dynamic closure** and the **correlated structure** of value estimates in offline settings. The Besov dynamic closure generalizes prior dynamic conditions and accommodates inhomogeneous smoothness. The paper highlights the challenges of data reuse in offline FQI (Fitted Q-Iteration) algorithms due to data dependency, addressing it with a "double uniform convergence argument" and leveraging local Rademacher complexities. This work differentiates itself from approaches relying on data splitting (Yang et al., 2019) or potentially improper analysis of correlated structures (Le et al., 2019), offering a tighter dependence on the effective horizon $H$ in long-horizon scenarios.

### Rust (1997) - "Numerical Dynamic Programming in Economics" (Survey)
This comprehensive survey provides a foundational overview of **numerical methods for solving DP problems**, extensively discussing the "curse of dimensionality" in both discrete and continuous MDPs. It categorizes methods into discrete and smooth approximation techniques. Rust emphasizes that the curse of dimensionality is an inherent aspect of continuous MDPs, unavoidable by any algorithm in the worst case using deterministic methods. However, it explores three avenues to circumvent it:
1.  **Restriction to limited classes** (e.g., linear-quadratic MDPs).
2.  **Algorithms performing well on average-case basis**.
3.  **Randomized algorithms**.
The survey details methods like successive approximations, policy iteration, linear programming, and various function approximation schemes (piecewise linear/cubic interpolation, Chebyshev polynomials, neural networks). Crucially, it highlights that **randomized algorithms (Monte Carlo)** can break the curse of dimensionality for **Discrete Decision Processes (DDPs)** (finite action sets) by transforming multivariate integration from intractable to tractable. However, this does not extend to **Continuous Decision Processes (CDPs)** due to the intractability of non-convex multivariate optimization. The paper also discusses the role of "low discrepancy" grid points for average-case tractability in integration.

## Synthesis and Connections:

The reviewed papers collectively underscore the pervasive nature of the curse of dimensionality in sequential decision-making. A primary theme is the reliance on **structural assumptions** about the MDP or the value function to achieve tractability. Du et al.'s Bilinear Classes, Lu et al.'s Approximate Factorization, and Rust's survey all exemplify this, by either identifying underlying low-rank structures, decomposable dynamics, or specific classes of MDPs (DDPs, LQ-MDPs) that lend themselves to more efficient solutions.

**Function approximation** emerges as a critical tool, particularly deep neural networks. Liu et al. and Nguyen-Tang et. al. both delve into the theoretical guarantees of deep ReLU networks within Besov and Barron spaces, demonstrating their capacity to handle complex, high-dimensional functions. The choice of function space directly impacts the ability to circumvent dimensionality issues; for instance, Barron spaces are shown to avoid the curse for two-layer networks in Liu et al. The rigorous analysis of generalization errors, often mediated by Rademacher complexities and uniform convergence arguments, is central to these theoretical guarantees.

**Sampling efficiency** is another recurring motif. Lu et al.'s synchronous sampling and Rust's discussion of randomized algorithms (Monte Carlo) and low-discrepancy sequences highlight strategies to obtain meaningful information with fewer samples, thereby combating the exponential data requirements. The distinction between **online and offline learning** paradigms (Liu et al. vs. Nguyen-Tang et al.) further emphasizes how data availability and collection strategies influence methodological choices and theoretical bounds. Challenges unique to offline settings, such as correlated data structures and distributional shift (Nguyen-Tang et al.), necessitate specialized analytical tools like double uniform convergence arguments.

The overarching goal across these works is the pursuit of **provable sample and computational efficiency**. While the classical view (Rust) often highlights fundamental intractability in worst-case scenarios for general MDPs, newer frameworks (Du et al., Lu et al., Liu et al., Nguyen-Tang et al.) propose leveraging specific structural properties, advanced function approximators, and refined analytical techniques to push the boundaries of what is provably learnable. This includes moving beyond linear function approximation, rigorous treatment of non-i.i.d. data, and developing algorithms that scale with inherent problem complexity rather than raw dimensionality.

## Conclusion:

The curse of dimensionality remains a formidable challenge, yet significant progress is being made through a multi-faceted research agenda. The integration of sophisticated structural insights (e.g., Bilinear Classes, Approximate Factorization), advanced function approximation theories (e.g., deep ReLU networks in Besov/Barron spaces), and innovative sampling methodologies (e.g., synchronous, randomized, low-discrepancy) offers promising avenues. Future work will likely continue to explore these intersections, striving for robust, provably efficient algorithms that bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees and the demands of large-scale, real-world RL applications. The development of frameworks that explicitly account for model misspecification, correlated data, and non-i.i.d. settings will be crucial for the continued advancement of RL in high-dimensional domains.
