# Animal Intelligence: An Experimental Study of the Associate Processes in Animals (Thorndike, 1898)

## The Problem (Layperson)

How do animals learn? In the late 19th century, stories abounded of clever cats and dogs performing seemingly intelligent feats. Pet owners told tales of animals that appeared to reason, to understand human speech, to solve problems through insight. But these were anecdotes, not science. Thorndike wanted to know: when a cat learns to escape from a box, or a dog learns to come when called, what is actually happening in the animal's mind? Is the animal thinking, reasoning, forming ideas? Or is something simpler occurring?

The practical difficulty was that no one had actually measured animal learning systematically. Observations were collected haphazardly, often by people eager to attribute intelligence to their pets. The most remarkable behaviors were recorded while the ordinary ones were ignored. Thorndike set out to replace speculation with measurement.

## What Didn't Work (Alternatives)

Before Thorndike, comparative psychology relied on anecdotes. Observers would record instances of apparently intelligent animal behavior without controlling conditions or measuring learning curves. This approach had several fatal flaws: observers were biased toward finding intelligence; only unusual behaviors were recorded; the animal's prior history was unknown; conditions could not be replicated; and a single case was taken to represent an entire species.

The anecdotal method could not distinguish between animals that reasoned through problems versus those that stumbled upon solutions through trial and error. When a cat escaped from a box by pulling a loop, was it because the cat understood the mechanical relationship between loop and latch, or because it happened to claw at the loop while struggling and remembered the result?

## The Key Insight

Learning could be studied experimentally by placing animals in controlled situations and measuring how their behavior changed with practice. Rather than asking whether an animal "understood" or "reasoned," one could measure the time to success across repeated trials. If an animal understood the solution, it should succeed immediately after discovering it. If learning was gradual, something else was happening.

Thorndike's deeper insight, which he formulated as the Law of Effect, was that successful behaviors become associated with their triggering situations. When an animal in a particular situation performs an action followed by satisfaction, the connection between situation and action is strengthened. This selective retention of successful behaviors explains learning without requiring the animal to understand why the behavior works.

## The Method

Thorndike constructed "puzzle boxes" with various escape mechanisms: loops of string to pull, levers to press, platforms to step on, buttons to push. Hungry cats were placed inside with food visible outside. Their behavior was observed and the time to escape was recorded for each trial.

The learning curve was the primary datum. If a cat understood the mechanism, escape time should drop suddenly to near-zero after the first success. If learning was associative, escape time should decrease gradually across many trials as the successful movement became more strongly connected to the situation.

The experimental design controlled for prior experience (naive animals), motivation (standardized hunger), and individual differences (multiple subjects). By using many different escape mechanisms across many animals, Thorndike could study association formation in general rather than responses to particular stimuli.

## The Result

Cats learned gradually. The time to escape decreased in a smooth curve across trials, not in a sudden step. Thorndike's graphs showed unmistakable evidence of trial-and-error learning. Animals did not reason their way to solutions; they stumbled upon them and gradually stamped in the successful responses.

The specificity of learning was notable. A cat that learned to escape by pulling a loop did not immediately generalize to pressing a lever, even when both mechanisms operated the same latch. Each situation-response association had to be learned separately.

## Worked Example

Consider a cat in a box with a loop of string hanging inside. The cat can escape by pulling the loop, which releases the door latch.

Trial 1: The cat scratches, bites, pushes against walls. After 3 minutes of random struggling, a paw happens to catch the loop. Door opens. Time: 180 seconds.

Trial 2: Similar struggling, but the cat spends more time near where the loop hangs. Loop caught after 90 seconds.

Trial 5: Cat goes fairly directly to the loop area but still makes some irrelevant movements. Time: 45 seconds.

Trial 20: Cat immediately pulls the loop upon entering the box. Time: 6 seconds.

The learning curve shows gradual improvement, not sudden insight. The cat never "understands" that pulling the loop releases the latch. It simply develops a strong association: this box + pull loop = escape. The irrelevant movements gradually drop out while the successful movement becomes more firmly connected to the situation.

## Subtleties

The Law of Effect has an important asymmetry that Thorndike initially missed. He originally proposed that both satisfying and annoying consequences affected learning: satisfaction stamps in responses while annoyance stamps them out. In 1929, he revised this view after finding that punishment was much less effective at eliminating behaviors than reward was at establishing them. This asymmetry between positive and negative feedback remains relevant to modern reinforcement learning algorithm design.

Thorndike's notion of "situation" was left deliberately vague. In modern terms, we would ask: what is the state representation? Thorndike's cats presumably represented the situation through multiple sensory modalities. The puzzle box looked, smelled, and felt a certain way. Which features of this complex sensory input became associated with the successful response?

The gradual learning curves could arise from two different processes: gradual strengthening of a single situation-response association, or gradual elimination of competing responses. Thorndike's method could not distinguish these, and both may occur simultaneously.

## Critical Debates

Thorndike's interpretation was challenged by Gestalt psychologists, notably Wolfgang Kohler, who observed apparently insightful problem-solving in chimpanzees. Kohler argued that Thorndike's puzzle boxes were designed to prevent insight by hiding the mechanical relationships from view. When relationships were made visible, animals could solve problems through understanding rather than blind trial and error.

The debate between associative and cognitive accounts of learning continues in modern psychology and neuroscience. Model-free reinforcement learning, which learns values and policies directly from experience, echoes Thorndike's trial-and-error account. Model-based reinforcement learning, which learns a model of the environment and uses it to plan, echoes the cognitive tradition.

A deeper question concerns what counts as understanding. Thorndike's cats learned to pull loops without understanding mechanisms. But their behavior was perfectly adapted to their situation. In what sense does this differ from human "understanding," which may itself be a form of learned association, albeit more complex?

## Key Quotes

"Of several responses made to the same situation, those which are accompanied or closely followed by satisfaction to the animal will, other things being equal, be more firmly connected with the situation, so that, when it recurs, they will be more likely to recur." (p. 244, The Law of Effect as formulated in Animal Intelligence, 1911)

"The cat does not look over the situation, much less think it over, and then decide what to do. It bursts out at once into the activities which instinct and experience have connected with the situation 'confinement when hungry with food outside.'" (p. 45)

"In the first place, most of the books do not give us a psychology, but rather a eulogy, of animals. They have all been about animal intelligence, never about animal stupidity." (p. 4)
