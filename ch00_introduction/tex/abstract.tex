Dynamic programming provides the mathematical foundation for sequential decision-making under uncertainty, but the curse of dimensionality limits its practical reach. Reinforcement learning offers scalable alternatives, extending tractability to high-dimensional states, continuous actions, and strategic interactions. This survey introduces these methods to economists, reviewing the theory that connects dynamic programming to modern learning algorithms and demonstrating their power through worked examples in pricing, inventory control, strategic games, and preference elicitation. We also note the limits of reinforcement learning: lack of convergence guarantees, instability, brittleness, sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters, and reliance on accurate simulators. A companion survey \citep{rust2026structural} covers the inverse problem: inferring preferences from observed behavior.
