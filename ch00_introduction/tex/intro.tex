This survey introduces reinforcement learning to economists. We develop the theoretical connections between dynamic programming and reinforcement learning, demonstrating how value iteration, Q-learning, and policy gradient methods address variants of the same optimization problem. We then examine applications across several domains: control problems including pricing and inventory management; structural economic models with high-dimensional state spaces; strategic games in which multi-agent algorithms compute equilibria under imperfect information; bandit problems in which economic structure yields tighter regret bounds; and preference learning in which the Bradley-Terry model corresponds to discrete choice econometrics. The exposition combines formal development with computational illustration.

Dynamic programming provides the mathematical foundation for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. From optimal stopping to dynamic games, economists formulate choices as solutions to Bellman equations. The curse of dimensionality, however, constrains the scope of tractable problems. When state spaces are high-dimensional, action spaces continuous, or strategic interactions complex, backward induction becomes computationally infeasible.

Reinforcement learning extends dynamic programming to such settings. The underlying Bellman equations remain identical; the computational approach differs. Rather than computing value functions exactly over the entire state space, RL algorithms estimate them from sampled transitions. Rather than requiring explicit enumeration, RL approximates through function fitting. This methodological connection reflects parallel intellectual developments: dynamic programming in operations research evolved alongside reinforcement learning in artificial intelligence, with substantive exchange at several historical junctures.

These approximation methods sacrifice theoretical guarantees. RL algorithms lack the convergence assurances of exact dynamic programming. They exhibit sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization. They can converge to suboptimal policies without diagnostic indication. This survey presents reinforcement learning as a computationally powerful framework while acknowledging its methodological limitations.

The survey addresses the forward problem: computing optimal policies given a known or simulated environment. A companion survey treats the inverse problem of inferring preferences from observed behavior. Chapter 1 traces the parallel historical development of dynamic programming and reinforcement learning. Chapter 2 develops unified theory connecting planning and learning. Chapters 3 and 4 apply reinforcement learning to control problems and economic models. Chapter 5 examines strategic games. Chapter 6 addresses bandit problems. Chapter 7 discusses reinforcement learning from human feedback. Chapter 8 concludes.
